What are these contests?

Cowconuts, Geometry Bee, and School MATHCOUNTS are Local competitions in the Trivalley area. The 2nd and 3rd competitions were for middle schoolers in the PUSD. "Winner" refers to first place.

State MATHCOUNTS is a state-wide math competition (for each state) for middle schoolers.

The AIME is an invitational exam that (approximately) 5000 high school students from around the nation qualify for through the AMC.

The USAJMO is an invitational olympiad that (approximately) 250 high school students grade 10 or under from the nation qualify for through the AMC and AIME.

Oddities

There are a couple of strange things about my teaching style compared to "normal" tutors, so I should get them out there.

Philosophy: Not philosophy in the "very old Greek dudes" sense, but philosophy in the "there are some things that cannot be explicitly defined but will make you better at math." Despite being what might appear to be a cold and logical discipline, learning math and getting better at it actually requires being human.

Contest math: Sorry, but I really cannot resist giving people some contest math questions (and teaching in a similar fashion to contest math). I think that most people really suck at learning/teaching math because they've never taken a "deep dive" into it. I promise that this will be helpful to you. There are two types of problems I will give you: Check-in problems and contest problems (or problem problems). Your book only gives you the first type. If you don't solve problem problems, how are you actually supposed to learn anything?

Effort: You should probably put a good amount of it in. But in all honesty, I don't actually expect that I will need to put a ton in. Here's the reason: Check-in problems are either so boring/non-unique that I can make them up off the top of my head or are classic problems that I remember, and I have a repository of contest problems. (This is why I don't think getting tutored by me will be anything special, because at some point people just know the material well enough that they don't need any outside references to teach it.)

Blame your textbook: A lot of people think it's their fault they can't learn math well in school. It's actually not the vast majority of the time. In fact, the only real "mistake" is being ignorant that better materials exist. (This is usually contest math stuff.) It doesn't matter what your school textbook is: it sucks and is probably holding you back significantly. So find anything else. (Feel free to get in touch with me for suggestions.)
